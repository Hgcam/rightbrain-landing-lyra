How AI is shifting the operating model for marketing agencies

Most marketing agencies have experimented with AI. But there is a meaningful difference between individuals using AI and your business operating on it, and that gap is where the next operating model is being built.

FR Florian RuspiHead of Growth, Rightbrain 25 Jun 2026 7 min read

Most marketing agencies have experimented with AI. A tool here, a prompt there, a team member who has quietly built a few shortcuts into their weekly routine. The experiments are real and the results are often promising. However, there is a meaningful difference between individuals using AI and your business operating on it.

AI-native B2B services are attracting serious investment attention right now, and the underlying logic is straightforward. For every $1 spent on software, $6 is spent on the services that surround it (Sequoia). The problem has always been that agencies are hard to scale — margins are thin, delivery is manual, and growth has historically meant hiring. AI changes that equation, which is why capital is moving toward it.

Not all of that capital is moving toward agencies. A new class of competitor is being built, funded specifically to sell marketing outputs directly to brands: automated content platforms, AI media buyers, programmatic creative engines that produce assets for in-house teams without an agency in the loop. These are not tools being offered to agencies. They are alternatives to agencies, and they are targeting the routine, repeatable work first — performance reporting, content variants, long-tail assets, keyword search. The work that fills most of an agency's week, even if it is not where the agency believes it creates the most value.

The agencies that will hold their ground, and grow through this shift, are the ones that move that intelligence layer inside their own operations before the alternatives become the default.

What changes when agents are in the picture

AI tools, used individually, make individuals faster. AI agents, embedded into how the team actually works, change what the team is capable of altogether.

A general-purpose AI assistant sits outside the workflow. A team member opens it, inputs a task, copies the output, and moves on. This is useful, but fragile. It depends entirely on the individual's initiative, their prompting ability, and whether they remember to use it at all.

An agent, by contrast, runs inside the tools the team already uses. It fires when a trigger happens. A call ends and the transcript becomes a CRM update, a meeting summary, and a follow-up draft without anyone initiating it. A brief lands and the relevant account history, competitive context, and audience data are already assembled before a strategist opens the file. A campaign report is drafted from live platform data and ready for an account manager to review rather than build.

The work does not move faster because people are working faster. It moves faster because preparation, pattern-matching, and production happen automatically, and human time is concentrated on the judgement and direction those agents cannot replicate.

Intelligence work compresses into agents. Judgement and creativity expand to fill the space it creates.

Where agents create the most leverage in a marketing agency

The highest-value use cases tend to cluster around the work that is both repetitive and consequential: the tasks that happen every week, consume significant time, and produce outputs that directly affect client relationships.

Campaign reporting sits at the top of that list for most agencies. Platform data exported, formatted, interpreted, written up, and sent — the same structure, the same sources, the same format every week, per client, per account manager. When a reporting agent connects to those data sources and produces a structured draft, the account manager stops building reports and starts editing them. Hours saved per client per week, without any reduction in quality.

Brief development and call intelligence follow closely. A brief-writing agent that takes a client input and converts it into a structured strategic and creative brief, using the agency's own format and frameworks, removes a consistent point of friction from planning. A call intelligence agent that converts transcripts into CRM updates, action items, and follow-up drafts ensures the intelligence from every client conversation becomes a compounding asset rather than a recording nobody re-watches.

New business research, copy variant generation, audience analysis, and competitive monitoring are all meaningful candidates. The common thread is that the work is clearly defined, runs frequently, and produces outputs that a human reviews rather than starts from scratch.

The compounding argument

There is a temptation to frame the case for AI agents as an efficiency argument: fewer hours, lower cost, faster delivery. Those outcomes are real, but they undersell what is actually at stake.

The more important outcomes compound. Every agent an agency builds and refines gets better over time. Every workflow embedded raises the floor of what the team can deliver as a baseline. Every integration connected reduces the manual data movement that currently slows delivery down. The agency that started six months earlier is orders of magnitude more effective — its workflows refined, feedback loops tightened, integrations deepened. A late mover does not start from the same position and catch up. They start from behind, on ground that keeps shifting.

The agencies that move first will deliver at platform speed on the commoditised layer and compete on everything those point-solution platforms cannot replicate: strategy, brand thinking, creative direction, client relationships, taste, and the institutional context that builds with every year of working together.

The governance question most agencies skip

One of the most common failure modes in agency AI adoption is individual cleverness replacing institutional capability. Someone on the team is very good with prompts. They have built something useful for their own accounts, but it exists only for them, only on their machine, in a tab no one else visits. When they leave, it leaves with them.

Agents that compound are agents that are owned, versioned, and governed. The same quality across every account, not just the ones where someone happens to be skilled. Leadership has visibility into what is running, what it is producing, and where it is improving. Feedback from the team flows back into iteration. The agent library becomes infrastructure, not a personal toolkit. This is the difference between an agency that has experimented with AI and one that operates with it.

How Rightbrain helps marketing agencies make the shift

Agents are built using an intuitive visual builder that works for both technical and non-technical teams, so the people closest to the work can shape what the agents actually do. No-code templates and single-click deployment mean the time between identifying a use case and having something running is measured in days, not months.

The platform is model-agnostic, so agencies are not locked to a single AI provider as the market evolves. Each agent can run the model that performs best for its specific task, and side-by-side model comparison makes that selection a data-driven decision rather than a guess.

Governance is built in rather than bolted on. Role-based access controls mean the right people can use and manage each agent without creating compliance exposure. End-to-end monitoring gives leadership visibility into every agent's performance — usage, cost, outputs, and issues — in a single dashboard. Events can be replayed, failures traced, and improvements made on evidence rather than assumption. Integration connects agents to the tools the agency already relies on. Webhooks trigger agents automatically when events happen in connected platforms. API integrations allow agents to read from and write to CRMs, project management systems, and media platforms without manual data transfer. Work flows through the operating layer rather than around it. The result is an agency that does not simply use AI. It operates with it, at scale, with the governance and visibility that makes that sustainable.
